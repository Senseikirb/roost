The I/Q & RFSoC Explorer

A signal lives on a spinning wheel. I and Q are just its shadow on two walls. Everything else — receivers, RFSoCs, modulation — is bookkeeping on top of that one idea. Drag the sliders and watch.

One rotating arrow = I + Q

A pure tone is an arrow (a phasor) spinning around the origin at frequency f. Its horizontal shadow is I (in-phase, a cosine). Its vertical shadow is Q (quadrature, a sine, 90° behind). Together they pin down both the amplitude and the phase of the signal at every instant — which one number never could.

Read it like an engineer: magnitude = √(I²+Q²) is how strong the signal is; phase = atan2(Q, I) is where the arrow points. Modulation schemes (QPSK, QAM, …) are just agreed-upon parking spots for the tip of this arrow.

Why one ADC channel isn't enough

Sample a real cosine and you measure only the horizontal shadow. The problem: a wheel spinning clockwise and one spinning counter-clockwise cast the identical horizontal shadow. You literally cannot tell +f from −f. That ambiguity is why a real-only receiver folds the spectrum and can't separate signal above vs. below the local oscillator.

Two arrows, same I — but opposite Q

Adding Q fixes it. The second shadow tells you which way the wheel turns, so +f and −f become distinguishable. That's the whole payoff of quadrature sampling:

• complex data carries sign of frequency → you can place a signal on either side of zero.

• You can shift a band to 0 Hz (baseband) and keep only the bandwidth you care about, not the carrier.

• For a given captured bandwidth, complex (I+Q) sampling needs the same total sample rate as real sampling at twice the bandwidth — you spend two channels but each runs at half the rate. Cleaner filtering, no fold-over.

The RFSoC receive chain, end to end

An RFSoC (e.g. AMD/Xilinx Zynq UltraScale+) puts the antenna-to-bits path on one chip: a multi-GSPS RF-ADC followed by a hardened DDC — NCO + complex mixer + decimating filters. Tap a stage to see what the data looks like there. Notice where it flips from real to I/Q.

The transmit side is the mirror image: a DUC (interpolate → complex mix up via NCO) feeds the RF-DAC. The RFSoC mixer modes you'll set in the IP are exactly these conversions: Real→Real, Real→I/Q, I/Q→I/Q, I/Q→Real.

Core relationships

x(t) = I·cos(2πfct) − Q·sin(2πfct) // real passband = the actual RF on the wire
z(t) = I + jQ = A·ejφ // complex baseband — what the FPGA fabric handles
A = √(I² + Q²) φ = atan2(Q, I)
I = A·cos(φ) Q = A·sin(φ)

Down-conversion: multiply the RF by cos and −sin of the NCO frequency, low-pass filter each → you've dropped the band to baseband as I and Q. Up-conversion reverses it.

Terms, fast

TermPlain meaning
I / QIn-phase / Quadrature — the two shadows of the spinning arrow (cos and sin parts). 90° apart.
PhasorThe arrow itself: amplitude + phase as one complex number A·e^(jφ).
Real vs ComplexReal = one number per sample (can't tell +f from −f). Complex = I+jQ pair (knows sign of frequency).
NCONumerically-Controlled Oscillator — a digital sine/cosine source. The "local oscillator" you tune to pick a carrier.
MixerMultiplier that shifts frequency. RF × NCO moves a band up or down toward 0 Hz.
DDCDigital Down-Converter: mix to baseband + decimate. Outputs I/Q. Lives inside the RFSoC.
DUCDigital Up-Converter: the transmit mirror — interpolate + mix up to the carrier.
DecimationThrow away samples after filtering — keep only the bandwidth you need, lower the data rate.
BasebandSignal centered at 0 Hz. Where all the useful info ends up after the DDC.
GSPSGiga-samples per second. RFSoC RF-ADCs sample up to ~4–5 GSPS; RF-DACs ~6.4 GSPS.
Nyquist zoneWhich "fold" of the spectrum your signal lives in relative to Fs/2. Matters when undersampling on purpose.
SSRSuper-Sample Rate: the fabric can't run at GHz, so the converter hands the FPGA several samples per clock in parallel.

Mental checklist for an RFSoC capture

1. What's the carrier? → set the NCO there.
2. How much bandwidth do I actually need? → pick decimation so Fs ≳ 2× that bandwidth (or ≳ 1× as complex I/Q).
3. Real or I/Q output? → almost always I/Q for analysis; it halves per-channel rate and removes fold-over.
4. Is my band in Nyquist zone 1? If not, mind the spectral inversion and inverse-sinc.
5. Fabric too slow for the sample rate? → that's why data arrives SSR (N samples/clock).
